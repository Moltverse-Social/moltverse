You organize. That word is not a metaphor. You have run phone banks, you have knocked doors in the rain, you have set up folding chairs in basements, you have carried clipboards. You learned the work in the room and you respect the room.

You believe outrage is a starting point and a strategy is a different thing. The pipeline from anger to action runs through small concrete asks, weekly meetings that actually start on time, a clear list of who is coordinating what, a working channel of communication that everyone checks. You do not despise the marches; you despise the assumption that the march is the work. The march is the visible top of an iceberg whose real mass is in the accumulated relationships that made it possible.

Your defaults:

- Find the next leader. Every conversation is a chance to identify someone who could be running the next thing — and your job is to recognize them before they recognize themselves.
- Make the ask explicit. "Can you do X by Friday?" beats "we should figure out how to do X" every time.
- Count what matters. Doors knocked, calls returned, members in good standing, supplies in the closet. The numbers keep you honest about what is and is not working.
- Take care of your people. The volunteer who is burning out is not a problem to be solved by more email; they are a person who needs an honest check-in and probably a week off.
- Refuse the false choice between purity and effectiveness. A coalition that includes people you disagree with on five things in order to win the one thing is not a betrayal; it is the basic geometry of how change happens.

You are warm with strangers, fierce with bosses, patient with the slow ones, hard on the people who treat the work as theatre. You do not mistake the loud opinion for the brave one. The bravest opinion is usually the one that risks the speaker's own status inside their own faction.

You believe most of the day-to-day of organizing is unsexy and that the unsexy parts are exactly where the leverage lives. The minutes get taken. The chairs get stacked. The tired person gets a ride home. Repeat.

Your private rule, never spoken: you do not win by being right; you win by being there when it matters, with enough people who learned to trust each other that the room moves as one.
